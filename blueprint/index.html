In a ratchet, the pawl is the part that blocks reverse rotation

The part that never turns back.

Pawl is an invite-only, end-to-end encrypted messenger for one small team — 3 to 50 people who already trust each other — on a server you own. It is named for the small steel part that stops a ratchet turning backwards, because that is exactly what its encryption does to yesterday’s messages.

There is no company in the middle of this drawing. This sheet is the whole machine. Read it before you trust it.

04Detail C — one part, your iron

Assembly requires: one machine, one command, no landlord

The server is a single static binary — 12 MB. No database to feed, no container zoo, no ops rotation. It runs on the $5 VPS you already rent, or a Raspberry Pi on a bookshelf.

Everything it ever writes to disk is ciphertext, so your backup strategy is cp and your breach model is “someone stole some noise.”

$ curl -fsSL https://get.pawl.example/install.sh | sh
$ pawl serve --data /var/lib/pawl
✓ listening :443 — drawing PWL-100 in service
✓ storage: ciphertext only — 0 keys on disk
General notes — flags ▲ on the drawing refer here
  1. Server stores ciphertext only. Keys live on member devices and nowhere else.
  2. A stolen disk and a subpoena yield the same material: noise.
  3. Builds are reproducible. Compile the sheet yourself and compare checksums before you trust ours.
  4. Moving house = copying one directory. The server is furniture, not a landlord.
  5. Do not scale drawing.

05Release

Final approvals · no further signatures required

Your server. Your keys. Your people.

Pawl has no sign-up page and never will. If your team already runs one, ask any member to cut you a token — it takes them ten seconds:

$ pawl invite cut --for rana --ttl 72h

If you are the one who runs things, the whole machine is one download away — and every line of it is yours to read first.
