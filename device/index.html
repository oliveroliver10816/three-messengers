Operator card

Every control does one honest thing.

Nothing on the panel is decoration. Each label above maps to something the software actually does — here is the legend.

READOUT

Sealed before it leaves your hand

Every message is locked with X3DH and the Double Ratchet, re-keyed as you type. The server — your server — only ever holds ciphertext. It cannot read the traffic even if you ask it nicely.

KEYS

Verify people, not accounts

Every device carries its own key. Compare safety numbers or scan a QR fingerprint when you meet — and if anyone's key ever changes, Longwave gets loud about it, not quiet.

SYNC

Built for distance

Write on a train in Poland; it arrives when Perth wakes up. Offline queue, delta sync, and a low-band mode that drops to text when someone's on hotel wifi at the end of the world.

VANISH

Gone is gone

Set a timer per channel, burn-on-read for the sensitive stuff, or throw the global vanish for the day you need everything gone everywhere — every device, at once.

SEALED SND

Starve the metadata

Envelopes are sealed before routing, so the box can't say who is writing to whom. No phone numbers exist anywhere in the system — there is nothing to leak, sell, or subpoena.

SEVER

Lose a laptop, not the crew

From any device you still hold, revoke a lost one. Its keys die instantly and a remote wipe is queued for the moment it next touches the net. The rest of the crew never breaks stride.

232 bytes on disk · no names · no numbers · no social graph

Your crew reads the left window. Whoever holds the server — even you — reads the right one. A stolen disk, a curious datacenter tech, a subpoena: they all get the same noise.

Calls ride the same wire. Voice, video, and screen share are encrypted end to end and relayed by the binary you host — they never touch a company server, because there isn't one.

Installation

You own the iron.

Longwave is one static binary, about 12 MB, with no dependencies. It runs happily on a $5 VPS or the Raspberry Pi taped behind your bookshelf. First boot generates the house keys on your disk — not ours. There is no “ours.”

Nobody can sign up. You mint an invite token, hand it to someone you already trust, and revoke it any time — before or after they use it. No phone numbers, no directory, no door for a stranger to knock on.

And because storage is zero-knowledge, backups are boring: copy the ciphertext anywhere. Only your crew's devices can ever turn it back into words.

your-box · ssh
$ curl -LO longwave.example/lw && ./lw up

lw 1.0 · house keys generated on this machine listening :7443 · storage: ciphertext only

$ ./lw invite --ttl 72h --single-use
