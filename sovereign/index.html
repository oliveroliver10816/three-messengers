The Constitution

We, the three to fifty, in order to form a more private union, do ordain a wire of our own: our server, our keys, our people — and grant the rest of the internet nothing, not even our names.

Preamble · binding wherever the citizens stand
I

The Border

No public sign-up. No phone number. No strangers, ever.

Citizenship is a cryptographic invite token signed by a current citizen — and revoked the moment trust ends. The door has no handle on the outside.

Art. I · entry & exile
II

The Cipher

Every message is sealed before it travels. Always. By default.

X3DH agrees the keys; the Double Ratchet turns them with every message. Keys never leave your hardware. The wire carries envelopes — it has never once seen a letter.

Art. II · encryption of record
III

The Archive

Storage is zero-knowledge. A stolen disk is a brick of noise.

A subpoena returns the same noise, notarized. Your history exists only on the devices of the citizens — nowhere else has anything worth taking.

Art. III · what a raid recovers: nothing
IV

The Census

Every citizen, every device, its own key.

Verify each other by QR safety number — in person when you can. If a key ever changes, Enklav announces it loudly and blocks nothing quietly. A lost phone is struck from the census and remote-wiped.

Art. IV · verification & the lost device
V

The Silence

Sealed sender. Starved metadata. Timed ash.

The server cannot say who talks to whom, or how often — what is never recorded can never leak. Channels burn on a timer, messages burn on read, and one command vanishes everything, everywhere.

Art. V · the right to be unrecorded
VI

The Record

The whole state is open source, with reproducible builds.

The binary you run provably matches the code you can read — build it yourself and compare hashes. Telemetry: none. There is no capital to report back to.

Article VII — large enough to need its own page

The Territory

A nation needs land. Yours is 71 megabytes.

Enklav is one static binary. It annexes a $5 VPS, a Raspberry Pi on a bookshelf, the old laptop in a basement in Lagos. No Docker, no database to babysit, no ops team — one command and the border is up.

The same box relays your voice, video and screen share, queues dispatches for citizens who are offline, and delta-syncs the stragglers when their train leaves the tunnel. Move the whole country to new soil with one file. Nobody outside will notice.

The server is not our product. It is your soil. We can't read it, rent it, or lose it in an acquisition — we were never on it.
